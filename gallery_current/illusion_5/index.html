Explanation

We know that we rely on cues like the relative size of objects and linear perspective to perceive depth. We also know that objects that are far away tend to look like they’re moving more slowly than objects that are nearby in a car ride, for example. In this experiment, we sought to pair this latter apparent-speed cue with one of our other studied depth cues and see what happened to our depth perception when these two conflicted. Therefore, we created a series of videos showing two rows of dots, one of which moved more quickly than the other, and applied other depth cues one-by-one to explore whether one depth cue would overpower another or whether a bistable illusion would be formed.

In “No Competing Cues”, the two rows are in the same place in the visual field and have equal qualities. We expect that the slower row will appear to be behind the faster row.

In “Big Is Slow” and “Small Is Slow”, we varied which of the rows had bigger dots compared to the other. In “Big Is Slow”, there’s conflicting depth cues because objects that are far away typically appear both smaller and slower.

In “Blurred Is Fast” and “Blurred Is Slow”, we tried to replicate an aerial effect in each of the rows. We did this by blurring the dots of the selected row while trying to keep them looking the same size as the other row. Although bluer colors farther away could also be part of the aerial perspective, for the purposes of this experiment, we didn’t vary color because we didn’t want people to decipher depth by looking at which color dominated when the dots of different rows overlapped.

In the next four videos, we adjusted which row was higher in the visual field and by how much compared to the other row. This is because things that are farther away tend to be higher in the visual field.

In the “Opposite Directions…” videos, we sought to analyze whether objects need to be moving in the same direction in order to lead their relative speeds to serve as a depth cue. When we’re looking out the window during a car ride, everything (that’s not also a car) appears to move in the same direction relative to us, so we wanted to see how direction of movement and speed could compete and whether the depth cue fell apart if we split up speed and direction.

In the “Linear Perspective…” videos, we changed the scene to have lines that converged near the top, and we adjusted which of the rows was higher. We found that our perception of the row closer to the bottom of the screen being closer in depth to us was so strong that it impacted our perception of the speeds of the rows, especially for “Linear Perspective, Higher Is Slow”, where it felt like the rows were moving at the same speed.

When we ran through each of these videos ourselves, we found that some cues completely overpowered everything else. Namely, no matter whether they conflicted with the speeds of the rows or not, the relative size cue, the cue that faraway objects are higher in the visual field, and linear perspective determined which row appeared closer in depth to us. We found that our depth cues completely broke down in the “Opposite Directions…” videos, and we either experienced bistable or otherwise unstable perceptions in the rest.

Explanation of Design: In order to actually create the videos, we recorded our screens while we played a moving animation in Powerpoint. A limitation of this design is that Powerpoint tends to accelerate and decelerate movement in animations in the beginning and end. We tried to crop out the parts of the video where this happened, but it’s possible that there were still remnants of this left that affected our perceptions.
