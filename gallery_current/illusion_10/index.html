Spring 2023 Vision Lab

Do results similar to that of the horizontal and vertical McCollough effect occur with diagonal gratings of various orientations?

Olivia Joseph, Ella Tubbs, & Omer Eissa

The McCollough Effect is a visual perception phenomenon in which black-and-white gratings appear colorful contingent on the orientation of the gratings. Traditionally, after gazing between a pink horizontal grating and a green vertical grating for a minute, a black-and-white vertical grating will appear slightly pink and a black-and-white horizontal grating will appear slightly green.

McCollough hypothesized that this aftereffect was due to orientation-sensitive neurons in the lower, monocular regions of the visual cortex that could adapt to color. However, little research has been done to test whether orientations that aren’t exactly horizontal or vertical produce the same effect. Specifically, whether various degrees of diagonal orientations accomplish this.

For our visual illusion lab, we will explore this by creating three separate sets of diagonal grating orientations, and test the strength of the after-effect on diagonal orthogonal gratings. Do results similar to that of the horizontal and vertical McCollough effect occur with diagonal gratings of various orientations?

To provide a resolution to this question, we will use standard 45-degree diagonal gratings orthogonal to each other. For the training gratings, we will provide three different conditions.

For each training condition, gaze between the two colored gratings for 60 seconds. Then, observe the testing stimuli at the bottom of the page. Take a 60 second break in between test conditions.
