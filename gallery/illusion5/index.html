Can local linear depth perception cause impossible linear perspectives to look normal?

Eli Kramer, David Koplow, Hannah Collins.

We will be investigating depth cues from linear perspectives and how they can be used to create “impossible perspectives.” Our illusion consists of several different examples of converging lines that look like hallways extending away from the viewer:


Here, the second row is the inverted version of the first row, and in this case the “hallways” look like they are extending downhill versus uphill. Viewers will notice that each of the “hallways” look believable, despite only one perspective actually being possible. The hallways also look plausible together, but there is no physical viewing position in which one could simultaneuously see the linear perspectives of multiple hallways, directly.

What perspective do you think is correct? How confident are you? Were there any you deemed definitely impossible? Does the entire image of all the hallways an orientations feel incorrect at a glance? What about when you look at it longer?

Press the button below to show which one is correct.


As can be seen by the image above, only the B hallways have proper convergence.

Viewers will also notice that the shown perspective of each row of hallways is impossible, but they will still be able to perceive each hallway and its depth. Taken together, the components of this illusion illustrate the filling-in power of linear perspective (even with bad convergence), and how humans compute depth locally without requiring global consistency. Additionally, the global image doesn't feel incorrect (even if logically we are able to figure out it is), despite the viewer needing to be in different locations to view each hall way at the shown angle. This result aligns with other findings about our perceptual system, such as illumination inconsistencies that appear real but could not plausibly exist in the real world.
