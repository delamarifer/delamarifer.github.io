Understanding effect of motion on the persistence of the Herman-Herring grid illusion

Kinan Martin and Tharindu Withanage

The Herman-Herring grid is a classic illusion that is thought to be produced by the interactions of center surround receptive fields, although the cause of the effect is debatable. Observers see darker dots at the intersections of a white grid on a black background, and lighter dots at the intersections of a black grid on a white background. While the persistence of the illusion by changing the size and color of the grid have been studied before, effects of grid motion on the illusion have not to the best of our knowledge, which is the aim of our project.

To study motion, we created a Herman-Herring grid using the p5.js library and observed effects of expansion and contraction, rotations, and translations at varying speeds. We also studied effects of color variation, color oscillation, and size changes in combination with different motions to see any changes.

Try playing around with the different sliders to get an idea of what they do and how they affect the grid. Which individual parameters affect the illusion? Does the illusion break down at certain motion speeds? Does the intensity difference of the two colors affect the presence of the dots? Does altering multiple parameters result in interesting effects?

Let the grid rotate and look at it for about 30 seconds. Make the grid stop rotating abruptly. Do you notice anything interesting? Now let the grid rotate at a low speed. Slowly increase the color oscillation rate. Does something interesting happen to the motion of the grid at some point?
