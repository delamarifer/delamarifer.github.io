Is color used as a motion grouping cue?

Jesse, Katherine and Kartik

This illusion aims to explore whether the moving plaids example works with color.

Refresher on plaids: Recall from lecture that plaids are a common visual stimuli in perception experiments, and one of the ways they are used is in studying motion perception. The standard moving plaids stimulus has two sine wave gratings drifting across the screen in two different directions. The illusory percept is that the motion is in the combined (diagonal) direction, despite neither of the individual stimuli moving that way. This is governed by the intersection of constraints (IOC) model, which posits that the local motion information from each grating imply a specific motion in one dimension, giving a constraint line of possible velocities. The combination of the two gratings provides two individual constraint lines, and the perceived motion has a velocity correlating to the intersection point of the lines.

Plaids and color? Those experiments were done using only grayscale gratings. Our submission aims to investigate whether colored gratings maintain or disrupt the percept of combined motion. It is not immediately clear what would happen. Color and motion are largely separate streams of information from the bipolar cells in the retina (midget and parasol cells, respectively), through the retinal ganglion cells and LGN (parvocellular and magnocellular pathways), up to cortex and the higher association areas (ventral and dorsal streamsâ€“ the "what" and "where" pathways of visual processing). However, we also know that the higher level visual areas combine multiple kinds of visual information, and that the plaids motion perception is seen in neurons in higher levels of the visual system.

Our illusion. In the table below, the first row shows "control" (grayscale) stimuli. The following rows show the same stimuli but with colored gratings (both the same color), and finally stimuli that combine the two colors (different gratings are different colors). We keep the velocity and contrast the same for all conditions, but vary the spatial frequency (columns).

If color were to break the illusion, the last two rows would be the most likely cases to do so - especially the red/blue row, since the two plaids are stimulating completely different cones (L/S). This is analogous to how with the original grayscale plaids for different spatial frequencies between the two gratings- larger differences in spatial frequency do not produce the illusory motion and instead favor the slower velocity percept (as expected using Bayesian inference with a prior favoring slow velocities). However, this is complicated by the fact that spatial frequencies are a feature extracted in lower level vision, whereas color and motion have higher cortical regions for processing.
