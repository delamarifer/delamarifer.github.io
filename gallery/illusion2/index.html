Does the shape-hole illusion maintain consistency in various edge contour-boundary combinations?

Akila Saravanan, Ariadne Dulchinos, Harini Desikan


Design Explanation:

As addressed in the paper the main principles at play in our illusion are shape from shading and the influence of edge contours on lightness perception of an object. We set up our illusion with three different shapes: circles, squares, and triangles. The pairs of shapes are assigned a gradient: either top down or bottom up apart from that the shapes are completely identical. The shapes are also assigned different colors, to test additionally whether the perception of the illusion is potentially stronger/weaker based on the color. In the background we have a solid medium-gray or medium-color background as a control and gradient backgrounds: top down and bottom up as the experimental combinations. By using different shapes with varied numbers of corners and curved versus jagged edge contours we hope to cover a broad enough variety to test for global but also local continuity in experiencing the shape from shade illusion.

We hypothesize that changing shapes, especially those with corners, may cause local spots of the shape to either break or maintain the illusion against the background because of differences in the continuity of boundaries and contours. In layman’s terms this means that the lighting of the gradient background when contrasted against the gradient of the foreground shapes might make it less obvious for the viewer to perceive depth and the forms of convexity and concavity of the shape. In terms of the different colors we hypothesize that changing the color will most likely preserve the illusion because the color perception would not be a result of a different light source. A gradient in the same direction but with a different rate may still preserve the illusion because the light source would be consistent and simply provide a different shading pattern. Though the intensity of illusion could be better perceived depending on extraneous factors such as the color tuning on someone’s computer screen, whether they have the blue light filter on or off, and the brightness settings.
